# [The route]{.mark}

Salace river is widely considered one of most interesting and beautiful rivers in the Baltics for kayaking: well winding, with some rapids, and scenic cliffs and caves. 

The route is 22 km long and will take 5-6 hours.

## Costs

- Kayak rent ([25]{.mark} EUR per boat) - vs 30 EUR per person(!) in Estonia last year! Talk about inflation. Welcome to Latvia.

- Camping site rent ([10]{.mark} EUR per tent). If you don't plan to stay in a tent overnight, you don't pay.

- Common food and provisions - e.g. firewood, trash bags, water (\~[10-12]{.mark} EUR per person but you can opt out of the food part)

- Sauna is not confirmed yet, as unfortunately the campsite's sauna is reserved. If we managed to find a portable sauna to be delivered, I'll update on the costs and we'll choose whether to get one. 

- Personal snacks, food & drinks (depends on you)

- Getting there (petrol, car rent)

## Preparation

#### Accommodation and transport

For those staying overnight in tents at the camping site, the most important is: **tent** and **sleeping bag**. You can agree with someone to share the tent, but you need to have your own sleeping bag. If you don't have these - I'm sure it's easy to find by asking your friends, colleagues, or in #all-estonia :) A yoga mat or a special mattress (usually inflatable) for more comfortable sleep is nice for extra comfort. Some people tend to get cold if there's nothing under the sleeping bag, although if the night is warm it's usually fine without.

It was effective and convenient, but a massive brainfuck for the organisers 😅 If you have a spare spot in a tent: offer it up in the chat. If you are looking for a spot - ask for one. Although likely we'll make a little subpage that shows who has tents and who doesn't.**

When it comes to transport - we *will* still oversee it centrally: recruit enough drivers, and make sure all the seats are assigned (details have been posted in chat). Otherwise the outcomes are very suboptimal and there is a risk of people not having a seat to go back home in.

#### Key items to take (but this is not ALL; read on)

During the ride you'll likely have to occasionally get out of the boat in random places, and the bottom of the river might be rocky. So a mandatory piece of equipment is **slippers** (a lot of people use Crocs and find them very comfortable for this). You can also get special rubber water shoes.

Hopefully the weather will be hot and sunny, so certainly take **sunscreen**. Sometimes there are tons of mosquitoes and other little friends, both during the day and especially during the evening. So take some **repellent**.

We'll arrange some shared things (e.g. grill and everything for it), full checklist for what you may consider taking with you - below.

# The process

### Day 1

For the drive to the meeting point, assume 15 min for every person you need to pick up, and add another 30 min to what GMaps/Waze shows. There is **always** a technical stop and a coffee break :) A common bias is thinking that there can be a 5-minute stop at the gas station. That is a delusion. Always factor in 20-30 minutes.

We all meet at the set place (starting point of the trip), take out from cars what we need for the kayak ride. The drivers then take the cars to the camping site and are brought back by the boat guy (there's a van with 6-8 seats, hopefully two vans) together with the boats. If they can't arrange 2 vans, they offered to give us drivers that can take the remaining cars themselves using our keys.

While waiting for the drivers to return, we just chill - this will take about 45 minutes (it's 20 min each way). When we arrive at the camping site at the end of the day, our cars will be there, so you can have access to all the stuff, charge your phones and whatnot.

Now, we get into boats and get going. Every boat will have two safety vests. [Unlike last year, this year there **are drybags** provided with the boats.]{.mark} We highly recommend you use one (or feel free to get one of your own - for instance, at Decathlon; the ones the give are probably not top quality). You can safely put your phones, dry clothes, towels, food and drinks there. Some don't use it at all - up to you. If you have a special waterproof transparent case for your phone - that's great. Not only for your phone, but also for the river, which is still decomposing someone's iPhone from last year... :(

The ride will take anywhere between 4 and 6 hours (this very much depends on how full the river is on that date, as well as how you make the trade-off between rowing and chilling). Some prefer to barely row and just chill in the boat, going with the flow - literally. During the journey we make pit-stops in accessible parts of the coast, check out the sights, meet nature's needs, or even walk around in nearby parks or settlements if there are any. You can also stay in the boat and go further - up to you; the main thing - don't get lost :)

In some parts of the route we **might** have to get out of the boats, and carry them some 30-50 meters by hand to cross impassable points. On Vohandu there were two such points, the OGs will remember the wooden rail after the ice cream shop. On Salaca - probably none, we'll see.

We arrive at the campsite (usually before 8 PM), park the boats. We take out all the camping equipment from the cars, set the tents up. Someone makes fire, someone starts cooking, someone starts drinking or goes for a nap - whatever you like. Although be prepared to volunteer to take some of the shared responsibilities. For instance, if firewood is not available at the campsite - someone goes for wood. During the cooking and after dinner we usually gather by the fire and play something, or just chat, or whatever floats your boat (hehe). All entertainment is optional :)

We don't yet know if at the campsite there is drinking water (although bottled one tastes much better - we'll buy some big bottles). But there's a fire pit with the possibility to add grill grid or cauldron, toilet, electricity sockets. Unfortunately there are no showers, but there is a beautiful clean river.

Sauna-on-wheels is to be confirmed. Nothing better than those refreshing dips. The sauna costs X EUR, so we'll split that between everyone who wants to use the sauna (trust me you should!).

### Day 2

We have breakfast, pack up the tents, tidy up the camping site, pack the garbage, say goodbyes and head home.

# Provisions

What you'll eat and drink during the ride depends on you and you can decide on your own what to consume. Remember - there is no fridge, so while on the first day you can grab some perishables, by the second day they'll probably be gone. Sharing is part of the process, so you can count on others to share some goodies :)

You'll definitely get hungry in the nature: the boat is quite spacious so it's best to take some extra stuff and finish it later.

### Recommendations

- Minimum 1.5 litres of water per person per day

- All sorts of dry snacks - sausages, cheese, crisps, light snacks, veggies

- Some take whole containers with sandwiches

- Sweet stuff that doesn't melt: buns, biscuits

- Enjoyable drinks besides water: alcohol also falls into this category. If you drink, definitely take a few beers/ciders into the boat - there's usually a convenient holder in the seat, and nothing beats sipping a beer while admiring Latvian nature on a river 🙂It's best to take it in cans not bottles - it's lighter, and cans don't drown as much as glass bottles.

### Food for the evening

While we hope you'll be properly supplied on your own with snacks for the boat, in the evening it's nice to cook something common. We'll probably take plenty of grill meat for everyone, some vegetables, perhaps bake potatoes or whatnot. We can also make the Eastern European classic (assuming we find a cauldron) - "pasta Navy", which is pasta with canned meat. So unless you have special dietary restrictions, for dinner you can rely on common food.

If you have any great recipe ideas - please share :)

For breakfast on day 2, usually people make some instant coffee, instant noodles, or basic sandwiches. There will be at least one portable gas stove which we can boil water in, so you can count on that. Disposable plates and cutlery should be left from the night before as well, but if you have camping-friendly ones, best take them.

### Medications

During the ride you might burn your skin, scratch yourself, get bitten by something, get wasted and suffer from hangover, or accidentally eat something wrong. Take some medicine and bandaids with you. Don't forget about any chronic things you might have - for instance, allergies.

### Rules of conduct and garbage

It goes without saying that we don't leave any garbage in the nature - we leave it in the boats and then collect into shared garbage bags. The camping site must stay as clean (or even cleaner) as it was before we arrived. We put out the fire(s). Everywhere we'll stop, only our footsteps can remain: we take all the trash with us or leave it in specially designated places. We don't burn plastics in the fire.
